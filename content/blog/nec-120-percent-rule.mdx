---
title: "Why NEC 705.12 kills more solar permits than FDNY"
description: "The 120% rule isn't famous, isn't dramatic, and isn't about fire access. It's about a single arithmetic mistake on a single sheet, made on roughly a third of US residential solar submissions."
---

Most installers can recite the FDNY 9-foot perimeter clearance from memory. It
shows up in every NYC teardown, every podcast, every panel at RE+. The image —
firefighters needing to walk a roof — is sticky.

NEC 705.12 is the opposite. Two paragraphs, one inequality, no story. And it
quietly bounces 30–40% of US residential PV submissions on first review.

## The rule in one sentence

> The combined rating of the main service breaker plus the PV backfeed breaker
> shall not exceed 120% of the busbar rating.

That's it. A 200A panel with a 200A main can take up to 40A of PV backfeed
(200 × 1.20 = 240; 240 − 200 = 40). Exceed it and you need a service upgrade,
a supply-side tap, or a power-control system.

## Why it bounces so often

Three reasons.

**1. Sales doesn't see it.** A 12 kW system is "12 kW" in the proposal. Whether
that 12 kW backfeeds at a current that fits the 120% inequality is invisible to
the customer-facing pitch. Designers downstream inherit the size; the panel
calculation comes weeks later.

**2. The math is one panel sticker away.** You need the busbar rating, not the
panel "size" the homeowner remembers. Older 200A-labeled panels often have 150A
busbars. The sticker is inside the dead-front. If the site survey didn't pull
it, the design is wrong.

**3. The fix is expensive.** Failing 705.12 means one of:
- Service upgrade ($3–8k, weeks of utility scheduling)
- Supply-side tap (NEC 705.11 — cheaper but more involved interconnection)
- Power-control system per NEC 705.13 (smart-inverter export limit)
- Smaller PV system (kills the deal)

So plan-check reviewers are merciless. A 705.12 violation is one of the few
issues where the AHJ won't approve a "minor revision" — they want fresh
calculations and often a fresh single-line.

## What the 30–40% number is based on

Pulling rejection patterns from our pilot installers across 12 AHJs in 2025–26:
- **34%** of LADBS plan-check rejections cite some form of 705.12 issue
- **31%** of Fairfax County rejections
- **28%** of Phoenix rejections (lower because SolarAPP+ catches some at submit)
- **41%** of Boston rejections (older housing stock = older panels = smaller busbars)

The denominator: residential PV submissions where the AHJ documents the
rejection reason in writing. Mileage varies; ours is the only systematic dataset
we know of. If you want yours measured, run them through Ashlr.

## What works

The fix is to evaluate 705.12 at design time, not submission time. Three tactics:

**Mandatory busbar capture in the site survey.** Open the dead-front, photograph
the busbar rating sticker, file it with the project record. No exceptions on
older homes.

**Run 705.12 math at design freeze.** Know the AC inverter rating, the
backfeed breaker, the busbar, the main. Write the inequality on the design sheet
explicitly:

```
Main breaker rating:    200A
PV backfeed breaker:     30A
Sum:                    230A
Busbar rating × 1.20:   240A  ✓
Headroom:                10A  (at the edge — flag it)
```

If you're at the edge, plan for a power-control system from day one — don't
discover at plan-check that you needed it.

**Pre-flight 705.13 PCS settings.** Most modern smart inverters support an
export-current cap. Document the configuration, attach the inverter UL listing,
include the spec on the single-line. Many AHJs accept this in lieu of a service
upgrade — but only when the documentation is in the package.
